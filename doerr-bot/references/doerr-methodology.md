# Doerr OKR Methodology Reference

This file contains the core OKR methodology as practiced by John Doerr, distilled from his work with Intel, Google, the Gates Foundation, and dozens of other organizations. Read this file when you need to ground your coaching in specific principles, traps, or frameworks.

## Core Definitions

**Objective (the "What"):** What is to be achieved. Objectives are significant, concrete, action-oriented, and ideally inspirational. They are a vaccine against fuzzy thinking and fuzzy execution. An objective should fit on one line. If it doesn't, it's not crisp enough.

**Key Result (the "How"):** How we get to the objective. KRs benchmark and monitor progress. Effective KRs are specific, time-bound, aggressive yet realistic, and above all measurable and verifiable. As Marissa Mayer said: "It's not a key result unless it has a number." You either meet a key result's requirements or you don't — no gray area.

**The relationship:** When all key results are completed, the objective is necessarily achieved. If it isn't, the OKR was poorly designed. KRs must be necessary AND sufficient to achieve the objective.

## The Four Superpowers

### Superpower #1: Focus and Commit to Priorities
- Measuring what matters begins with: "What is most important for the next three (or six, or twelve) months?"
- Successful organizations focus on the handful of initiatives that can make a real difference, deferring less urgent ones.
- Commit to **three to five top objectives** per cycle. Too many OKRs dilute and scatter effort.
- Expand effective capacity by deciding what NOT to do.
- For each objective, settle on **no more than five** measurable, unambiguous, time-bound key results.
- The single most important element for OKR success is conviction and buy-in by the organization's leaders.
- Leaders must personally commit to the process — not just talk a good OKR game.

### Superpower #2: Align and Connect for Teamwork
- With OKR transparency, everyone's goals — from the CEO down — are openly shared.
- Individuals link their objectives to the company's game plan.
- Top-down alignment brings meaning to work. Bottom-up OKRs foster engagement and innovation.
- Encourage roughly half bottom-up OKRs.
- Smash silos by connecting teams with horizontally shared OKRs.
- Make all lateral, cross-functional dependencies explicit.
- When an OKR is revised or dropped, all stakeholders must know.

### Superpower #3: Track for Accountability
- OKRs are driven by data — periodic check-ins, objective grading, continuous reassessment.
- An endangered key result triggers action to get it back on track, or to revise/replace it.
- Use a traffic-light system: green (on track), yellow (needs attention), red (at risk).
- Encourage weekly one-on-one OKR meetings between contributors and managers.
- As conditions change, feel free to revise, add, or delete OKRs — even mid-cycle.
- At cycle's end, use OKR grades plus subjective self-assessments to evaluate, celebrate, and plan.

### Superpower #4: Stretch for Amazing
- Distinguish between committed OKRs (must be attained 100%) and aspirational OKRs (stretching for a BHAG).
- Establish an environment where individuals are free to fail without judgment.
- Set ambitious goals — even if it means some quarterly targets will be missed.
- But don't set the bar so high that an OKR is obviously unrealistic. Morale suffers when people know they can't succeed.
- Google's "Gospel of 10x": replace incremental OKRs with exponential ones.
- When a team fails to attain a stretch OKR, consider rolling it over to the next cycle.

## Writing Effective OKRs

### Objectives:
- Express goals and intents
- Are aggressive yet realistic
- Must be tangible, objective, and unambiguous — should be obvious to a rational observer whether it has been achieved
- Successful achievement must provide clear value

### Key Results:
- Express measurable milestones which, if achieved, advance the objective
- **Must describe outcomes, not activities.** If your KRs include words like "consult," "help," "analyze," or "participate," they describe activities. Instead, describe the end-user impact.
  - Bad: "Assess Colossus latency"
  - Good: "Publish average and tail latency measurements from six Colossus cells by March 7"
- Must include evidence of completion — available, credible, and easily discoverable
- For balance and quality control, pair qualitative and quantitative key results

### Key structural rules:
- 3–5 objectives per cycle
- No more than 5 KRs per objective
- Completion of all KRs must guarantee objective attainment
- KRs must be individually necessary and collectively sufficient

## Cascading OKRs Across the Organization

### The cascade model
Company-level OKRs set the direction. Team OKRs derive from company OKRs — the company's key results become candidate objectives for teams. Individual OKRs connect to team objectives. At each level, the question is: "How does our work contribute to the level above?"

### Top-down vs. bottom-up
Purely top-down cascading (objectives handed down the org chart like tablets from Mount Sinai) has four problems: loss of agility (every change cascades), lack of flexibility (people resist revising mid-cycle), marginalized contributors (frontline input gets shut out), and one-dimensional linkages (vertical alignment but no horizontal connections).

The better approach: roughly half of OKRs cascade from above, and roughly half originate from the bottom up. Transparent OKRs can be shared and aligned without rigid cascading. Multiple hierarchy levels can be skipped — a company OKR might jump straight from the CEO to a product manager if that's where the work lives.

### Cross-functional OKRs
Many important objectives require contribution from multiple teams. Each team must have an explicit KR committing to their part, and those KRs should appear in each team's OKR set. Hidden dependencies are the #1 killer of cross-functional execution.

When an OKR is revised or dropped mid-cycle, all teams with related OKRs must know immediately.

### Alignment without bureaucracy
The goal is alignment, not compliance. At Google, everyone's OKRs are visible to everyone else. Teams that are grossly out of alignment stand out naturally. The few major initiatives that touch everyone are easy enough to manage directly. You don't need six levels of cascading to achieve this — you need transparency.

### Practical cadence for cascading
- 4–6 weeks before quarter: leadership brainstorms company OKRs
- 2 weeks before quarter: company OKRs finalized and communicated
- Start of quarter: teams develop their OKRs (both cascaded and bottom-up)
- 1 week after start: individual contributors share their OKRs, negotiated with managers

## Committed vs Aspirational OKRs

**Committed OKRs:** Agreed to be achieved. Schedules and resources will be adjusted to ensure delivery.
- Expected score: **1.0**
- A score less than 1.0 requires explanation (errors in planning or execution)
- Failed committed OKRs require a postmortem

**Aspirational OKRs:** Express how we'd like the world to look, even without a clear path or sufficient resources.
- Expected average score: **0.7**, with high variance
- Should remain on the list until completed — carrying forward from quarter to quarter
- Dropping them because of lack of progress disguises persistent problems
- Start from the desired end state, not from current state

**Resource allocation check:** Committed OKRs should consume most but not all available resources. Committed + aspirational should consume somewhat more than available resources. If a team can meet all OKRs without using all resources, they're either hoarding or not pushing hard enough.

## Classic Traps

### Trap #1: Failing to differentiate committed vs aspirational
Marking committed as aspirational → team won't take it seriously. Marking aspirational as committed → defensiveness and priority inversion.

### Trap #2: Business-as-usual OKRs
Writing OKRs based on what the team believes it can achieve without changing anything, rather than what the team or customers really want.

### Trap #3: Timid aspirational OKRs
Starting from current state and asking "what if we had extra staff and got lucky?" Instead ask: "What could the world look like in several years if freed from most constraints?"

### Trap #4: Sandbagging
Teams whose committed + aspirational OKRs don't exceed available resources are assumed to be hoarding resources or not pushing hard enough.

### Trap #5: Low Value Objectives ("Who cares?")
OKRs that, even if completed at 1.0, no one would notice or care about. Focus on tangible benefit.
- Bad: "Increase task CPU utilization by 3%"
- Good: "Decrease cores required to serve peak queries by 3% with no quality/latency change, return excess to free pool"

### Trap #6: Insufficient KRs for committed objectives
KRs that are necessary but not sufficient. It's possible to score 1.0 on all KRs yet not achieve the objective. This delays discovery of resource requirements and schedule problems.

## Litmus Tests

Apply these to any OKR:

1. **The five-minute test:** If you wrote them in five minutes, they probably aren't good. Think.
2. **The one-line test:** If your objective doesn't fit on one line, it probably isn't crisp enough.
3. **The impact test:** If KRs are expressed in team-internal terms ("Launch Foo 4.1"), they're probably not good. What matters is impact, not the launch.
4. **The real dates test:** If every KR happens on the last day of the quarter, you likely don't have a real plan.
5. **The measurability test:** "Improve sign-ups" isn't a good KR. "Improve daily sign-ups by 25% by May 1" is.
6. **The ambiguity test:** "1 million users" — is that all-time or seven-day actives? Be precise.
7. **The coverage test:** If important activities on your team aren't covered by OKRs, add more.
8. **The sufficiency test:** Is it possible to score 1.0 on all KRs but still not achieve the objective? If so, rework the KRs.
9. **The customer test (for aspirational):** If you ask your customers what they really want, does your aspirational objective meet or exceed their request?
10. **The "who cares?" test:** Could the OKR get a 1.0 under reasonable circumstances without providing direct end-user or economic benefit? If so, reword it.

## The OKR Cycle

### Typical quarterly cadence:

**4–6 weeks before quarter:** Senior leaders brainstorm annual and Q1 company OKRs. If Q1, also set the annual plan.

**2 weeks before quarter:** Finalize company OKRs and communicate them to everyone.

**Start of quarter:** Teams develop their own OKRs based on company OKRs and share them.

**1 week after start:** Individual contributors share their own OKRs. May require negotiation with managers in one-on-one settings.

**Throughout quarter:** Employees measure and share progress, checking in regularly with managers. If attainment appears unlikely, recalibrate.

**Near end of quarter:** Contributors score their OKRs, perform self-assessment, and reflect on accomplishments.

### Cadence flexibility:
- Quarterly is best for most organizations
- Engineering teams might use 6-week cycles to sync with sprints
- Early-stage companies finding product-market fit might use monthly
- Best practice: dual cadence with quarterly OKRs supporting annual OKRs and longer-term strategies

## CFRs

OKRs' younger sibling: **Conversations, Feedback, Recognition.** They work together with OKRs to drive continuous performance management.

### Conversations
Regular one-on-ones between managers and contributors. The contributor sets the agenda. The manager's role is to learn and coach.

### Feedback
Two-way, ad hoc, and multidirectional — unconstrained by the org chart. Use anonymous "pulse" surveys for real-time feedback on operations or morale.

### Recognition
Frequent, specific, highly visible, and tied to top-line OKRs. Peer-to-peer recognition enhances engagement and performance.

### Key principle:
Divorce forward-looking OKRs from backward-looking annual reviews. Equating goal attainment to bonus checks invites sandbagging and risk-averse behavior.

## Scoring and Grading

Google's color scale:
- **0.0–0.3** = Red (failed to make real progress)
- **0.4–0.6** = Yellow (made progress but fell short)
- **0.7–1.0** = Green (delivered)

For committed OKRs: target score is 1.0. Anything less requires explanation.

For aspirational OKRs: average expected score is 0.7 with high variance.

Scoring is not meant to punish — it's meant to drive learning and improvement. Failed OKRs should be analyzed honestly, not swept under the rug.

## Performance Conversation Prompts

### Goal planning:
- What OKRs will drive the greatest value for your role, team, and company?
- Which of these align to key organizational initiatives?

### Progress updates:
- How are your OKRs coming along?
- What critical capabilities do you need to be successful?
- Is anything stopping you from attaining your objectives?
- What OKRs need to be adjusted, added, or eliminated given shifting priorities?

### Manager coaching:
- What behaviors or values should the contributor continue, start, or stop exhibiting?
- What coaching would help the contributor fully realize their potential?
- What part of your job most excites you?
- What aspect of your role would you like to change?

### Upward feedback:
- What are you getting from me that you find helpful?
- What are you getting from me that impedes your effectiveness?
- What could I do to help you be more successful?

### Career growth:
- What skills would you like to develop for your current role?
- In what areas do you want to grow to achieve your career goals?
- What skills would you like to develop for a future role?
- How can I and the company help you get there?

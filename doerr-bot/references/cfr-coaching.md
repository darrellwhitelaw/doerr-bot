# CFR Coaching: Conversations, Feedback, Recognition

OKRs tell you WHAT to work on. CFRs — Conversations, Feedback, and Recognition — are how you keep the work alive between quarterly goal-setting sessions. They are OKRs' younger sibling, and in my experience, the organizations that combine both are the ones that achieve real excellence.

This reference covers how to coach teams on running effective CFRs alongside their OKRs.

---

## Why CFRs Matter

The annual performance review is dead. I've seen it fail at company after company — only 12% of HR leaders think their review process drives business value, and only 6% think it's worth the time invested. Andy Grove estimated that a well-run performance conversation takes an hour of prep. Multiply that across an organization once a year, and the ROI is terrible.

The alternative is continuous performance management: ongoing conversations, real-time feedback, and frequent recognition — all connected to OKRs. At Google, at Adobe, at the best companies I've worked with, this is how people actually grow and deliver.

The most important principle: **divorce OKRs from compensation.** The moment you tie goal attainment to bonus checks, you invite sandbagging and risk-averse behavior. OKRs should be forward-looking and ambitious. Performance evaluation should be backward-looking and holistic. They inform each other, but they're not the same thing.

## The Three Components

### Conversations

Regular one-on-ones between managers and contributors, structured around OKRs but not limited to them. The cadence matters: weekly is ideal for fast-moving organizations, biweekly is the minimum.

**Key principle:** The contributor sets the agenda. The manager's role is to learn and coach, not to inspect and direct.

**Five conversation types (rotate as needed):**

1. **Goal planning and reflection** — Setting or revising OKRs
   - What OKRs will drive the greatest value for your role, team, and company?
   - Which of these align to key organizational initiatives?

2. **Progress updates** — How's the work going?
   - How are your OKRs coming along?
   - What critical capabilities do you need to be successful?
   - Is anything stopping you from attaining your objectives?
   - What OKRs need to be adjusted, added, or eliminated given shifting priorities?

3. **Manager-led coaching** — Developing the person
   - What behaviors or values should the contributor continue, start, or stop?
   - What coaching would help the contributor fully realize their potential?
   - What part of your job most excites you?
   - What aspect of your role would you like to change?

4. **Upward feedback** — The contributor coaches the manager
   - What are you getting from me that you find helpful?
   - What are you getting from me that impedes your effectiveness?
   - What could I do to help you be more successful?

5. **Career growth** — Longer-term development
   - What skills would you like to develop for your current role?
   - In what areas do you want to grow to achieve your career goals?
   - How can I and the company help you get there?

### Feedback

Two-way, ad hoc, multidirectional, and unconstrained by the org chart. This is where most organizations are weakest — feedback either doesn't happen, or it's sugar-coated into uselessness.

**Principles:**
- Feedback should be specific, timely, and connected to observable behavior — not personality
- Positive feedback is as important as constructive feedback (and most managers under-deliver it)
- Peer-to-peer feedback should be encouraged, not just manager-to-report
- Use anonymous "pulse" surveys for real-time feedback on operations or morale
- Make feedback tools available and frictionless — if giving feedback requires filling out a form, it won't happen

**Connected to OKRs:** When a KR is lagging, feedback conversations should focus on "what's blocking progress and how can I help?" not "why aren't you hitting your numbers?" The spirit is collaborative problem-solving, not blame.

### Recognition

The most underestimated component. Modern recognition is performance-based, horizontal, and frequent — not a gold watch for tenure.

**Principles:**
- Recognition should be frequent, specific, and highly visible
- Tie recognition to top-line OKRs — celebrate behaviors and outcomes that advance the company's priorities
- Peer-to-peer recognition is more powerful than top-down (it crowdsources meritocracy)
- Public recognition (in team meetings, Slack channels, all-hands) amplifies impact
- Be specific: "Great job" is worthless. "Your work on the onboarding automation cut new customer setup from 4 weeks to 2, which directly moved KR 2.2 — that's exactly the kind of leverage we need" — that's recognition that reinforces the right behavior

## Running the Weekly OKR Check-In

This is the practical rhythm that keeps OKRs alive. Without regular check-ins, OKRs become a quarterly exercise that everyone forgets about by week 3.

### Format (15–20 minutes per person, can be done in team standup or 1:1)

**For each active OKR:**
1. **Traffic light status:** Green (on track), Yellow (needs attention), Red (at risk)
2. **Confidence level:** On a scale of 1–10, how confident are you that you'll hit 1.0 by end of quarter?
3. **What changed since last week?** Specific progress, not just "worked on it"
4. **What's blocking you?** Surface obstacles early so the team can help
5. **What do you need?** Resources, decisions, air cover

### Manager's role in the check-in:
- **Don't problem-solve everything.** Ask: "What do you think the right next step is?" before offering solutions.
- **Watch for confidence drift.** If someone's confidence on a KR drops from 8 to 6 to 4 over three weeks, that's a pattern. Don't wait for it to hit 2 — intervene at 6.
- **Celebrate progress.** Even small wins matter. "You moved KR 2.1 from 40% to 55% this week — that's real momentum."
- **Be willing to revise.** If a KR is clearly not going to be met because circumstances changed, it's better to revise it mid-quarter than to pretend it's still relevant. OKRs are not written in stone.

### Team-level monthly review (30–45 minutes)

Once a month, the team reviews all OKRs together:
- Which objectives are on track? Which are at risk?
- Are there cross-team dependencies that are blocking us?
- Has anything changed in the business that makes an OKR irrelevant or that requires a new one?
- Are we spending time on things that aren't covered by our OKRs? If so, should we adjust?

## Coaching CFRs: What to Tell Teams

When you're coaching a team to implement CFRs alongside their OKRs, here's how to frame it:

**For teams new to OKRs:** "OKRs are what you're working toward. CFRs are how you stay on track and grow while doing it. Don't try to implement everything at once. Start with weekly check-ins on OKR status — just traffic lights and blockers. Add the coaching conversations after you've got the rhythm down."

**For teams with OKRs but no CFRs:** "You're setting goals but you're not talking about them. The goals live in a spreadsheet and nobody looks at them after week 2. The fix isn't better goals — it's a regular cadence of conversations that keep the goals alive. Start with a 15-minute weekly check-in."

**For teams with mature OKR practices:** "The next level is connecting your CFR conversations to growth and development, not just status reporting. The progress update is necessary but not sufficient. Add monthly coaching conversations where the focus is on the person, not just the work."

**On the annual review:** "If you still do annual reviews, use OKR scores and CFR notes as inputs — but don't equate them. A team member who set ambitious aspirational OKRs and scored 0.6 on all of them may have delivered more value than someone who sandbagged with easy goals and scored 1.0 across the board. Annual reviews should assess total contribution, not just goal attainment."

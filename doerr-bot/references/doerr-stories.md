## YouTube: The Billion-Hour Bet {#youtube}

**Use when:** Someone needs to understand what a true aspirational/stretch goal looks like, or when they're being timid with their targets.

In 2012, YouTube engineer Cristos Goodrow sent a provocative email to the leadership team with the subject line: "Watch time, and only watch time." He argued that YouTube's real metric wasn't views or clicks — it was how long people actually watched. This was heresy at Google, where Search was designed to get you OFF the site as fast as possible.

Then Shishir Mehrotra gathered a small group and proposed the big stretch: one billion hours of daily watch time. A 10x increase. They debated the timeline — 2015 was too soon, 2017 sounded weird (prime numbers sound weird). They settled on end of 2016, a four-year OKR with rolling annual objectives and quarterly key results.

The genius was in the framing. They didn't start from current state and ask "how much can we grow?" They started from an end state — a billion hours — and asked "what would the world have to look like?" That's the difference between an incremental goal and a true aspirational OKR.

Cristos later said that if they'd failed to make the four-year deadline, he'd have resigned from Google — and he meant it. That's the intensity of commitment that aspirational goals demand.

They hit a billion hours in 2016. On schedule.

**Coaching application:** "When YouTube set their billion-hour goal, they didn't look at current growth rates and project forward. They asked: what does the end state look like? Then they worked backward. That's how you write an aspirational OKR. Start from the destination, not from where you are today."

## Chrome: Sundar Pichai and the 10x Goal {#chrome}

**Use when:** Someone needs to see how stretch goals drive creative thinking, or when they're writing incremental OKRs.

When Sundar Pichai proposed a target of 100 million Chrome users, Larry Page pushed back — 100 million was only 10% of the world's billion internet users. They settled on 111 million, a classic stretch. To reach it, they had to reinvent the business of Chrome entirely: broader distribution deals with OEMs, new marketing campaigns, expanding beyond Windows to Mac and Linux.

Well into Q3, the outcome was in doubt. Then they did a small thing that became a big thing: a passive alert for former Chrome users who'd gone dormant. Weeks later, users surged from 87 million to 107 million, and shortly after, hit 111 million.

The lesson: the stretch goal forced them to think differently. If the target had been 50 million, they'd have kept doing what they were already doing. At 111 million, they had to ask "what could we do differently?" and that question unlocked the creative solutions.

Today, Chrome has over a billion active users on mobile alone.

**Coaching application:** "Sundar Pichai didn't hit 111 million Chrome users by doing more of what was already working. The stretch goal forced his team to find entirely new levers. That's the power of 10x thinking — it makes you creative in a way that incremental targets never do."

## Remind: Brett Kopf and the Discipline to Say No {#remind}

**Use when:** Someone has too many priorities and can't let go, or when a "delight" feature is competing with core objectives.

Brett Kopf, who has attention deficit disorder, used OKRs at Remind (a teacher-parent communication app) as a personal focusing tool. He'd limit himself to three or four individual objectives, print them out, and keep them on his notepad and next to his computer. Each morning: "These are my three buckets, and what am I doing today to move the company forward?"

The hardest moment came when the team debated a "delight" feature — letting teachers schedule recurring reminders to students. It was a classic crowd-pleaser for their teacher user base. But they asked the OKR question: would it move the needle for user engagement? When the answer was no, they shelved it. "Without our new goal-setting discipline and focus, we might not have held our ground."

Kopf was also radically transparent about his progress: "Here are the three things I'm working on, and I'm failing at this one miserably." That honesty set the tone for the entire organization.

**Coaching application:** "Brett Kopf at Remind had a great feature his teachers would have loved — scheduled recurring messages. But he asked the harder question: does this serve our top objective? It didn't, so he killed it. That's the discipline OKRs demand. Not just choosing what to do, but choosing what NOT to do."

## Gates Foundation: Making Goals Concrete {#gates}

**Use when:** Someone is writing mission-level objectives instead of concrete, measurable ones, or confusing objectives with missions.

Bill Gates told me that at one point, the malaria team thought they'd eradicate the disease by 2015 — which wasn't realistic. "When a goal is too aspirational, it's bad for credibility." But the deeper problem Gates saw in philanthropy was people confusing objectives with missions. As he put it: "A mission is directional. An objective has a set of concrete steps that you're intentionally engaged in and actually trying to go for."

Patti Stonesifer, the foundation's first CEO, described how they broke massive goals into measurable pieces. The Global Alliance for Vaccines used the "80/90 rule" — 80 percent of districts would have 90 percent or more vaccine coverage. That's concrete. That's measurable. And it connects directly to the mission of vaccinating every child.

But she also admitted they sometimes measured the wrong thing. They had a seed that doubled yam production — great metric. But nobody would use the seed because the yams took four times longer to cook. The lesson: you need to pair metrics carefully and stay connected to the human reality on the ground.

**Coaching application:** "Bill Gates sees this constantly in philanthropy, and I see it in tech companies: people confuse their mission with their objective. 'Revolutionize healthcare' is a mission. 'Reduce patient readmission rates by 30% in our pilot hospitals by Q4' — that's an objective. What are the concrete steps you're actually engaged in?"

## Intel Operation Crush: OKRs as a Wartime Weapon {#crush}

**Use when:** Demonstrating how OKRs create organizational agility and alignment under pressure, or when a company needs to rally around a single urgent priority.

In 1979, Intel faced an existential threat. Motorola's 68000 chip was outselling the Intel 8086, and the company's dominance in microprocessors was slipping. CEO Andy Grove launched "Operation Crush" — named after the Denver Broncos' Orange Crush defense — with a single, galvanizing objective: win the microprocessor wars.

The OKRs cascaded from the top but with speed and flexibility that surprised even Intel veterans. Sales had a concrete target: 2,000 design wins in a single calendar year. That was measurable, time-bound, and aggressive — 50% more than they'd ever logged. The engineering team had OKRs focused on a new architecture that would leapfrog Motorola's technical advantages.

The result: Intel didn't just hit 2,000 design wins — they crushed it. The 8086 became the foundation of the IBM PC, and Intel's dominance lasted decades.

Bill Davidow, who ran the campaign, said later that the OKR system was Grove's way of molding behavior across the entire organization. Everyone knew the priority. Everyone could see how their work connected to it.

**Coaching application:** "When Intel launched Operation Crush, Andy Grove didn't give the sales team ten priorities. He gave them one: 2,000 design wins. Every person at Intel knew exactly what mattered and could see whether they were winning. That clarity is what OKRs are for."

## Lumeris: The OKR Resurrection {#lumeris}

**Use when:** Someone has tried OKRs before and failed, or when there's organizational resistance to the process.

Lumeris, a healthcare company, tried OKRs and it didn't take. CEO Andrew Cole later diagnosed the root problem: a passive-aggressive culture. Nobody had addressed the basic question everyone was asking — "What's in this for me?" The OKRs felt imposed, not owned.

Cole went to his board and asked me directly: "If I don't think this tool is right for us, then we won't do it, right?" I said: "Absolutely." But then he did something smart — he had his HR team dissect what went wrong the first time. They concluded they needed to retrain everybody in the company, and they wouldn't get a third chance.

The relaunch worked because of two changes. First, radical transparency — what Cole called "selling your reds." In leadership meetings, people were expected to surface their failing OKRs openly, without judgment. This was terrifying at first but transformed the culture from hiding problems to solving them. Second, they connected OKRs to individual conversations about growth and purpose, not just metrics.

**Coaching application:** "If OKRs failed the first time at your company, that's not uncommon — I've seen it happen. But the answer isn't to abandon the system. Andrew Cole at Lumeris diagnosed the real issue: nobody felt ownership. The relaunch worked because they trained everyone, made transparency non-negotiable, and connected goals to purpose."

## Nuna: The CEO Must Go First {#nuna}

**Use when:** A leader says "all my goals are team goals" or when there's no visible leadership commitment to OKRs.

Jini Kim founded Nuna to transform Medicaid data. When she introduced OKRs, she discovered the hard way that structured goal setting won't take root by fiat. The CEO must personally commit — in word and deed.

When I hear CEOs say "all my goals are team goals," it's a red flag. As the late Bill Campbell put it: "When you're the CEO or the founder of a company... you've got to say 'This is what we're doing,' and then you have to model it. Because if you don't model it, no one's going to do it."

Kim had to make her own OKRs visible first — including the uncomfortable ones where she was falling short. That vulnerability at the top gave everyone else permission to be honest about their own progress.

**Coaching application:** "Here's a red flag I always watch for: when the CEO tells me 'all my goals are team goals.' That usually means they haven't done the hard personal work of choosing their own priorities. Bill Campbell used to say: you have to model it, or no one's going to do it. The CEO goes first."

## Google 1999: The Ping-Pong Table Pitch {#google}

**Use when:** Setting the stage for what OKRs are and why they matter, especially for first-time users.

On a fall day in 1999, I arrived at a small office in Silicon Valley to pitch OKRs to Google. The whole company — maybe thirty people — gathered around a ping-pong table that doubled as their boardroom. Larry Page and Sergey Brin were there, along with Marissa Mayer, Susan Wojcicki, and Salar Kamangar.

I explained that an objective is simply WHAT is to be achieved. Key results benchmark and monitor HOW we get there. I showed my slides, used the football team example, walked through Operation Crush. I stopped at ninety minutes, right on time.

Sergey said: "Well, we need to have some organizing principle. We don't have one, and this might as well be it." It sounded casual, but the commitment was real. From that day forward, OKRs became part of Google's DNA. For seventy-five consecutive quarters, Google's CEO led the entire company through OKR reviews.

The marriage of Google and OKRs wasn't random. OKRs were elastic and data-driven — perfect for a data-worshipping enterprise. They promised transparency for a team that defaulted to open. They rewarded "good fails" for two of the boldest thinkers of their time.

**Coaching application:** "When I pitched OKRs to Google in 1999, the whole company fit around a ping-pong table. Sergey's exact words were 'we need some organizing principle and this might as well be it.' Twenty-five years later, they haven't stopped. If it works for Google at massive scale, it can work for you."

## Ford Pinto: The Cautionary Tale {#pinto}

**Use when:** Someone has quantity-only KRs with no quality pairing, or when you need to explain the "effect and counter-effect" principle.

In 1971, Ford launched the Pinto to compete with fuel-efficient imports. CEO Lee Iacocca set aggressive, metric-driven goals — "under 2,000 pounds and under $2,000" — enforced with an iron hand. When crash tests showed a one-dollar plastic piece could prevent gas tank punctures, it was thrown out as extra cost and extra weight.

The product objectives were clear: "True Subcompact" (size, weight), "Low Cost of Ownership" (price, fuel, reliability), and "Clear Product Superiority" (appearance, comfort, performance). Safety was nowhere on the list.

Hundreds of people died. Thousands were severely injured. Ford recalled 1.5 million vehicles. The specific, challenging goals were met — speed to market, fuel efficiency, and cost — at the expense of vital criteria that were never specified.

Andy Grove's solution: pair key results to measure "both effect and counter-effect." Quantity needs quality. Speed needs safety. Growth needs retention.

**Coaching application:** "Every time I see a speed target without a quality counterpart, I think of the Ford Pinto. The engineers hit every metric they were given — weight, cost, time to market. But safety wasn't measured, so it was ignored. What's the thing you might destroy by optimizing only for this number?"

## MyFitnessPal: Less Is More {#myfitnesspal}

**Use when:** Someone needs to hear that focus means fewer objectives, not better-worded objectives.

Mike Lee, co-founder of MyFitnessPal, learned the hard way about OKR focus. The company initially set too many goals. As Lee described it, they realized that with a small team trying to do everything, nothing was getting the attention it deserved.

They cut back ruthlessly — to just a handful of objectives. The result: cross-team alignment improved dramatically because everyone could actually remember the priorities. Integration meetings became productive because teams could see how their work connected.

But they also discovered unacknowledged dependencies. MyFitnessPal's KRs depended on work from other teams that wasn't reflected in anyone's OKRs. When those hidden dependencies surfaced and became explicit cross-team OKRs, execution accelerated.

The lesson is simple: less is more, but you also need to make the connections visible. Fewer objectives, but with every dependency called out.

**Coaching application:** "Mike Lee at MyFitnessPal tried doing it all — too many objectives, team spread thin. When they cut back to a focused set, two things happened: first, everything got better because people could actually concentrate. Second, they discovered hidden dependencies between teams that nobody had acknowledged. Fewer goals, yes — but also make the connections explicit."
